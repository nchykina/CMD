What is the reference genome?

When doing DNA realignment, we are always using a reference genome of the corresponding species, as we compare the information in the reads with it, looking for SNPs and Indels. But what exactly is this reference genome? Who does it belong to, and who assembled it?

Haploidity

Let's make it clear first what forms the human genome and how it is different from the DNA. The prevailing majority of cells in our body are diploid, which means that they have pairs of chromosomes, not a single copy of each non-sex chromosome. The exception are sex cells which are haploid , so each of them contains only 1 chromosome in each of the 22 pairs. So, when scientists are talking about a human DNA, they usually mean the whole set of the DNA sequences contained in a diploid cell: 22 pairs of non-sex chromosomes, 1 pair of sex chromosomes (XX for females and XY for males) and mitochondrial DNA that is located outside of the cell's nucleus.

Inclusion of both chromosomes in every pair allows to keep the information about homozygous and heterozygous alleles in the chromosomes. Homozygous allels are the ones that have the same nucleotide on the same loci on both chromosomes in a pair, while heterozygous alleles have different nucleotides on the same loci on each of the chromosomes (please see the picture on the right for clarification).

Image 1. Zygosity

So how is the reference human genome different from the DNA sequence? The key simplifying assumption that the bioinformaticians make here is that the reference genome is haploid, so it comprises only one chromosome per pair. In addition to 22 chromosomes, it includes both chrX and chrY (so technically, it's a male), mitochondrial DNA (chrM) and unaligned/unplaced sequences (will be discussed later).

You can easily see the problem that arises here. Say, the reference sequence had heterozygosity on a particular allele with A on one chromosome and T on another. Then, we have made the reference genome haploid and included only A-s in it. Afterwards, we take the reads from someone with the same heterozygosity on the same allele, and - voila - half of the reads show that there is an SNP on that allele, which is of course incorrect.

Sampling

Another good question is how the scientists came up with the sample of people to be sequenced for the reference genome. The exact answer depends on the version of genome that is used, but in general the samples have been pretty small and unrepresentative. For instance, the widely used reference genome version GRCh37 has been assembled from a very small sample of only 13 anonymous volunteers from Buffalo, New York!

Image 2. Variants map from 1000 Genomes

But why couldn't the scientists take a more diverse set of people for construction of the reference (at least, of different races, from different countries etc.) and take an average across this set of data to get a "reference human being"? Wouldn't it be more correct? Actually, it wouldn't. All people on Earth belong to certain clusters called haplogroups, and people within every such cluster have similar haplotypes. Haplotype is a combinations of nucleotide variants, or SNPs, that are likely to be inherited together. Map of common macro variations from the 1000 Genomes project is provided on the image on the left.

So, suppose we take a very diverse set of people representative of all the races and geographic areas and construct our reference genome by averaging up across the genomes of these people. What we will most likely get as a result is just a mess, as such a combination of SNPs may be contradictory and will never be inherited together (for instance, we can get an African American with blue eyes and blonde hair). So, when constructing a reference genome, it makes more sense to stay at least within the same haplotype. Also, when studying some diseases or traits that are specific for one of the haplotypes, it makes perfect sense to take as a reference the genome of people of that particular haplotype. In any case, the generic version of the reference human genome is highly skewed and based on an extremely small sample. But unfortunately we can hardly do a better one, so a good judgment should be made is every particular case based on the goal of the research and input data used.

Bureaucracy and the lack of a single standard

Many institutions and companies have over time published their own versions of the reference genomes. These versions differ from each other in naming conventions, as well as the data included, so we should understand at least at the high level, how are these versions different and which one should we use.

1. Human Genome Project

The Human Genome Project (HGP) was launched in 1990 with the goal of sequencing and identifying all three billion nucleotide pairs in the human genetic code. It was declared complete on April 14, 2003, when the final sequencing mapping was published. Most of the government-sponsored sequencing was performed in twenty universities and research centers in the US, the UK, Japan, France, Germany, and China. HGP, as any other project done afterwards, didn't sequence the genome of a particular individual. Instead, an HGP genome is the combined mosaic of a small number of anonymous donors, all of European origin.

It is interesting that a parallel private project was conducted outside of government by the Celera Genomics. This project was formally launched in 1998 by Craig Venter who was a scientist at the NIH in the early 1990s. Celera's funding was around USD 300 mln, and it planned to commercialize its efforts by seeking patent protection on the discovered genes. However, in March 2000, Bill Clinton announced that genes can't be patented and should be made freely available to scientific community. As a result of this announcement, Celera's stock price plummeted and biotechnology sector lost USD 50 bn in market capitalization within two days.

2. Genome Reference Consortium and GRCh37 genome

As HGP has been completed, it became clear that its results needed further research and improvement. This research is the core focus of Genome Reference Consortium (GRC), whose primary members are NCBI, Wellcome Trust Sanger Institute and European Bioinformatics Institute. In March 2009 GRC made the first major release of the human genome called GRCh37 .

The human genome in GRCh37 release comprises 24 chromosomal sequences (chromosomes 1 to 22, X and Y) and mitochondrial DNA sequence. In addition to that, it included several unlocalized sequences (those that are attributed to a particular chromosome, but their location on it is unknown) and unplaced sequences (those that couldn't have been aligned to a particular chromosome). Also, starting from the h37 release, GRC reference genome began to include the alternative loci - sequences that contain alternative representations of specific, highly variable human genome regions (such as major histocompatibility complex region, essential for the immune system).

In December 2013 a new version of the GRC reference genome (GRCh38) was submitted to GenBank. The new features in it include 261 alternative loci (most of which are located on highly variable areas in chr6 and chr 19), centromere representation, revised version of chrM and a number of sequencing errors fixes. GRC constantly issues further minor and major updates to its reference genome, as long as new information is being discovered.

3. UCSC hg19

Shortly after GRCh37 has been released, UCSC (University of California, Santa Cruz) released its own build of the reference genome called hg19. The key difference in this build is the mitochondrial DNA sequence, which is taken from the previous, GRCh36 genome version. Also, UCSC uses slightly different naming scheme for the chromosomes and unplaced/unlocalized sequences.

The hg19 build has gained popularity due to its availability via the UCSC genome browser, and is often the convention used by vendors when reporting exome enrichment kit coordinates. Also, Ion Torrent (one of the key producers of sequencing devices) has also issued its own version of UCSC's hg19 reference genome, with the only difference being that chrM is used from GRCh37, not GRCh36 build.

4. 1000 genomes project

1000 Genomes project took quite a different approach. It was launched in January 2008 and was aimed at sequencing the genomes of 1000 anonymous participants from different ethnic groups. The reference genome created by the project was called b37. It follows the naming conventions similar to GRC's ones, but doesn't include the alternative loci at all.

The International HapMap Project launched in 2002 had somewhat similar goal as 1000 Genomes, being aimed at creating a haplotype map of the human genome to describe the common patterns of human genetic variation. It used samples from 269 individuals that were genotyped; however, it didn't sequence the whole genome of every individual (it was still too costly at that time). Instead, the scientists selected several million well-defined SNPs and genotyped the individuals for them.

What's next?

Further advances in the reference genome area are constantly taking place, as different organizations continue the issuance of improved and more precise builds.

Also, in June 2016 HGP-Write mega-project has been officially announced, as a continuation of the HGP-Read project that has been completed. The goal of HGP-Write is to create a synthetic human genome within the next 10 years.
