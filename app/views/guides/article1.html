FASTA/FASTQ format

One of the first things you face as a newbie bioinformatician are files in FASTA/FASTQ format. They are a de facto standard for storing raw input data in bioinformatics and are widely used across the industry. So, what exactly do these files look like?

First, let's discuss where FASTA/FASTQ files come from and what kind of information they contain. FASTA/FASTQ files are generated by sequencing devices (Illumina MiSeq/HiSeq, Roche, Ion Proton etc.) that take DNA/RNA genetic material as an input. These devices ( sequencers) differ from each other by processing throughput, cost per run and other technical characteristics. Illumina is clearly the most popular sequencer brand that has nearly 90% of the market.

To understand FASTA format you should first grasp the basics of what is going on in the lab. In simple terms, the "magical" process that is happening at the facilities of a sequencing provider looks the following way:

1. NGS library preparation

Lab technician orders a library preparation kit , usually from the sequencing device manufacturer. This kit is used to prepare long nucleic acid chains (DNA or RNA) for high throughput sequencing process. Preparation kits vary a lot depending on many factors, but conceptually they usually comprise 3 kinds of chemical compounds: adapters, restriction enzymes and primers.

So, how do these chemical compounds work? At the first stage, restriction enzymes cut the long nucleotide chains of DNA or RNA into pieces of randomized length. This step is necessary because high throughput sequencing assumes reading many relatively short nucleotide chains (100-150 bases) in parallel.

Afterwards, the adapters come into play. These are short synthesized double-stranded DNA molecules with sticky ends that are attached to every DNA/RNA fragment at both ends. After the adapters are attached, the fragments are PCR-amplified (copied) to get more genetic material available for further processing.

Next, the lab technician takes a special flow cell. This flow cell has lanes with surface-bound short oligos that are complementary to the adapters. The DNA/RNA fragments are ligated (attached) to these oligos, each at one end (as shown on the picture).

2. Bridge amplification and clustering

The subsequent process of bridge amplification and clustering is built around the concept of iterative "reconstruction" of single-stranded DNA/RNA to double-stranded chains. This is done by dNTP molecules. dNTP (deoxyribose nucleoside triphosphate) is the common name of a molecule that consists of a nucleotide (A,T,C or G), three phosphate groups and deoxyribose.

This iterative process goes the following way: remember we had our segments attached at one end. So, the next thing we do is attaching them at the second end to another oligo from the flow cell to form a U-shaped "bridge". Then this single-stranded bridge is "repaired" by dNTP to a double stranded bridge chain and again broken into 2 separate strands. What we get as a result is 2 complementary sequences identical to each other but attached at different ends to the flow cell.

The process is repeated numerous times, and as a result we get "islands" (clusters) of segment copies and reverse complements, where all copies are attached at one end and all complements - at the other end. This whole process is called bridge amplification.

3. Attachment of primers and sequencing

Primers are used to mark the parts of DNA/RNA that are going to be sequenced, as in most cases you don't want to sequence the whole genome (it's very costly) but only particular parts of it. Primers possess chemical structure that allows them to bind to the specific sites on the genome marking these sites for further processing.
